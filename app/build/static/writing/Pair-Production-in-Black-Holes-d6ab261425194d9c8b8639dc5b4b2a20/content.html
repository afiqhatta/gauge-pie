When we look into the sky, we see that some galaxies emit powerful jets of light. In the 1970s there are a few explanations as to what these things actually were. The model that was use to explain powering galactic nuclei at the time was the existence of either a single super large pulsar, or a cluster of pulsars. However, these models posed problems. One problem was that there is a critical mass for neutron stars, and a neutron star large enough to emit the amount of light would be too big to exist.


One way that the paper [1] tries to resolve this problem, was to see if a black hole could emit jets of light. For this to happen, we need there to be particles near the magnetosphere of the black hole. Material that accretes near a black hole will probably be magnetised. Near the magnetosphere, there must be some source of particles. The mechanism to explain this is electron-positron pair production .


In the paper [1], they consider black holes which are magnetised, and that have an accretion disc. Currents flowing in an equatorial disc that support the magnetic field. This disc can be fuelled by gravity, but it can also be fuelled by magnetism. A plasma is a soup of charged particles. A force-free magnetosphere is a magnetic field with a negligible plasma pressure contribution, but a significant magnetic pressure contribution. To model this, we look at pulsars.
