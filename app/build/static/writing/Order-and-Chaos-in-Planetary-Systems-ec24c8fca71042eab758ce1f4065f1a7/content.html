Mean Motion Resonances in Planets

One of the things that Professor Terquem studies are systems of planets that have a ‘clean’ ratio of time of orbit. In particular, the periods of the orbit of some planets around a central body have ratios that are small integers. For example, Terquem and many others reference two of Saturn’s moons - Enceladus and Dione. It takes Dione approximately twice as long to orbit Saturn than Enceladus. In this case, the ratio would be 2:1. I found this remarkable because I wouldn’t expect a violent and chaotic process like planet formation to lead to a finely tuned result. Even though I am not an astrophysicist myself, I was surprised when I first read about this. The only thing that rang a bell was fractional and integer quantised values in the Hall effects from quantum mechanics. Even then, planetary systems are not quantum mechanical!


I read the paper’s abstract [3], and some questions came to mind about how this resonance might take shape. Imagine planets orbiting a star in the early stages of their creation. Sometimes, there is also a disk of gas orbiting the star itself, with the planets embedded inside - kind of like chocolate chunks in a cookie. By Newton’s third law, this disk of gas also exerts a force on the planets. These forces also cause the orbits of these planets to alter over time. The planets might move inward, towards the star, or move outward. During this process, it appears that planets get ‘locked in’ to these nice ratios of orbit periods. When and where does this resonance occur? Does it happen only in restricted areas of the disc? (Spoiler alert: after reading a bit more, these questions and more happen to be discussed in the paper, and I will write about it in a future post!)


True to the idea of order and disorder, the reverse can also happen. On the flip side, a system in this satisfying resonance can destabilise. This destabilisation is also an interesting question. If a system is in mean resonance, what kinds of factors can cause the planets to go out of sync? Are there any nice mathematical expressions that allow us to predict how quickly they can go out of sync? What regions of orbit in the disc does this destabilisation occur? Again, many exciting models are put forward in [3], and I wanted to write about them in a few posts.


There are also practical ways to observe this phenomenon. Kepler is a space telescope that uses photometry to capture the dynamics of planetary systems. The telescope works by looking at the slight dimming of stars when a planet orbits in front of them. In [2], a statistical analysis of Kepler data revealed that a small, significant portion of planets in the data had mean motion resonance. In [3], Caroline Terquem and John C. B. Papaloizou describe the dynamics of two-planet systems with an orbit period ratio of (q+1): q. They mention that the planets that Kepler captures tend to have shorter period orbits due to observational bias. These planets start further away from the star, and as they go in, they lock into this resonant pattern.


Governing equations

In [3], some nice properties like the eccentricity of the orbit of planets in mean motion resonance (MRR) are calculated. Eccentricity measures how squashed an orbit is. An eccentricity of zero represents a circle, and an eccentricity close to 1 means a trajectory that looks like an oval. When a system is in MRR, the dynamics are mainly governed by ‘perturbing functions’. For example, consider a system with one large mass (the central star) and two satellite planets. From these geometrical characteristics, we have a few derived quantities. The mean motion is the number of revolutions. It is the primary quantity of study when talking about mean motion resonances. Tau is the time where the orbiting planet hits the pericentre, and from this, we can derive two quantities called the mean longitude and the mean longitude.

It turns out that the disturbing functions can be written as a big Taylor expansion that is a function of terms involving the semi-major axes, eccentricity, reference angle and mean longitude. There is an essential mathematical quantity in orbital mechanics called eccentricity. In [3], some nice properties like the eccentricity of the orbit of planets in mean motion resonance (MRR) are calculated. Eccentricity measures how squashed an orbit is. An eccentricity of zero represents a circle, and an eccentricity close to 1 means the trajectory that looks like an oval. The eccentricity of an orbit can also be larger than one. If this is the case, the orbit is no longer closed and is a parabola instead - like a comet swerving around a plant once.
