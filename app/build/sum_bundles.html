Some problems in geometry

What does the Whitney sum bundle of a Mobius strip look like?

A Mobius trip cannot be parametrised globally by a set of coordinates on the product \( ( s, t ) \) since it is not a trivial bundle. However, we find that the Whitney sum of two Mobius bands is a trivial bundle.

The transition function associated with the Whitney sum of a Mobius trip in the top hemisphere of the circle is \( I \), and is \( - I \) in the bottom hemisphere. We need to show a change of coordinates which will trivialise this function everywhere. Just multiply by diag(1, -1), and diag(-1, 1), by setting a new coordinate local trivialisations to be defined as \( \tilde{ \phi \} _ 1 ( p ) = (t, -t) \) on the upper hemisphere, and the corresponding reverse map on the lower hemiphere. This gives \( g1 = diag(1, -1) \) , and the corresponding \( g_ 2 \) element. This change of coordinates allows us to transform our transition functions, and this should make the transition functions trivial everywhere. We cannot do this in the single dimensional case, since one of the charts will have to be taken to be the identity, which is a contradiction.

To construct the Whitney sum, we will show that the transition functions anywhere on the manifold can be taken to be trivial. Given some fibre bundle \( E \to B \), if there is a point in the intersection of two open covers \( p \in U _ i \cap U _ j \), then there exists two diffeomorphisms which between this and the fibre space. In other words, we have that \[ \phi _ i : U _ i \times F \to \pi ^ { - 1} ( U _ i ) \]

The transition function defined at the point \(p\) in the manifold is the function which maps the fibres associated with the diffeomorphisms to one another. For a bundle to be trival, we must be able to take these as the identity everywhere.

\[ t _ ij ( x) : F \to F \]

For a Mobius band, the two transition functions on the disjoint open sets are the identity and the sign flip functions respectively. For the Whitney sum, it is \( I , -I \).

If we have two vector bundles on a given space, we can construct a Whitney sum bundle, which is a space where at every point, the fibres look like a direct product of two vector spaces.

The simplest way to visualise a direct product is taking \( \mathbb{ R } \) as an example - the direct sum of the real line with itself is \( mathbb { R }^ 2 \). The Whitney sum bundle of a manifold is the construct that we get when we combinine two vector bundles such that at every point, the fibre is the direct sum \( V \oplus W\) of the two vector spaces.

Moreover, this fibration should preserve the properties of the original manifold. This means that, when we projecto down, we should still get the base space.

Constructing the Whitney Sum

A Mobius strip is constructed by making a unit interval \( I \) as the fibre over the circle \( S ^ 1 \). We take \( I \) as the vector space in this case, and we need to construct a map such that the two fibres form a direct sum.

To do this, take a circle. At each point \(p\) in the circle, we need to find a way to construct two fibres which have a direct sum locally. To do this, we parametrise going round the circle with \( \theta \in [ 0 , 2 \pi ] \), and assign two vectors \[ U _1 = ( \cos ( t / 2) , \sin ( t / 2) ), \quad U _ 2 ( - \sin ( t/ 2), \cos ( t/2 ) ) \] The first vector, as a line sub-bundle, traces the outside of a Mobius strip. It is a sub-bundle of a Mobius strip. Similarly, the second vector evolves the same way. Together, the fibre is isomorphic to \( \mathbb{ R } ^ 2 \), and so it is the trivial two dimensional tangent bundle on \( S ^ 1 \).

An alternative approach using transition functions

This can alternatively be shown by looking at the transition functions for the individual Mobius strips. A fibre bundle is trivial when we can take the transition function to be written as a factor of two structure group elements. \[ t_ {12} (x ) = g _ 1 ( x) g _ 2 ( x ) \] Without loss of generality we require that \( g _ 2 \) is a smooth map with \( g _ 2 ( x _ 1 ) = I, g_ 2 ( x_2 ) = - I \) on the place where the transition function is taken to not be the identity map on a single strip. However, this is a map in \( GL ( 2 , \mathbb{ R} ) \), so we're okay since this is connected. On the Mobius strip alone, the group \( Z _ 2 \) is not connected and not a Lie group.

For the case in which we are working with just a single Mobius band, we cannot call the group function \( g(x_1) = x_1, \, g(x_2) = - x \) trivialisable on the different sections of the mobius strip since this group is not connected, and so this operation is not smooth. However, we are at liberty to do this when we are working with the direct sum.
