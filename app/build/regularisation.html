Regularisation

The Casimir effect

If we are given a free scalar field theory, then the energy is encoded in the Hamiltonian. In momentum space, and in terms of the creation and annihilation operators, a Klein-Gordon theory has the Hamiltonian \[ H = \int d ^ 3 k \, \omega _ k ( a _ k ^ \dagger a _ k + \frac{ 1 } { 2 } )\] Contracting this Hamiltonian with the vacuum state \( \left| 0 \right\rangle \) annihilates the first term with the operators, but leaves us with the remaining expression \[ E \propto \int d ^ 3 k \approx \infty \] \( E \) here is the vacuum energy. It is clear that this vacuum energy can't be observable because it's infinite.

For now this is okay. But, how do we change this to get some observable phsyics out of this? Well, one thing that we can do is to contain this oscillator in a box and see what happens when we increase the size of the box.

Even after doing this, it still looks like the force on the walls of this constrained system is infinite, which doesn't agree with experiment at all. Thus, there's nothing wrong with the actual calculation itself, but something wrong with the physics. For example, with high wavelengths, these high frequency modes will just pass through the walls.

Resolving the discrepancies with the Experiment

To model this, we could for one try to model the interaction between the particles and the high frequency modes. However, this is probably a difficult thing to do. So, we can instead try to form some approximation. If different approximations lead us to the same result - then we've found something significant.

The approximation we will use to model the high frequency modes escaping this box is called a 'hard cutoff'. We model the energy contained in the box assuming that our high frequency modes are surpressed somehow. The first thing we could do is to regulate by enforcing a limit on the frequency. So, we may decide that \[ \omega < \Lambda r, \quad \omega_ n = \frac{ \pi }{ r} n \] Our maximum mode in this case obeys \[ n _{ max} ( r) = \lfloor \Lambda r \rfloor \] We derive the force from this regularisation by then substituting this into the expression for the energy, and then differentiating that. Casimir in 1948 predicted this force as \[ F( a) = - \frac{ \pi \hbar c }{ 24 a ^ 2 } \]

Regulator independence

For a wide variety of different procedures in which we could have chosen to choose a regularisation scheme, it turns out that for a lot of choices, we would have arrived at the same force. Casimir shows that this is true in his 1948 paper, that the Casimir force is independent of the choice of regulator subject to some conditions.

Counterterms

In physics, it is perfectly possible for our zero point energy to be infinite, since its not observable. To get physics out of the zero point energy, we need to place it in the context of wider space parameterised by some large boundary, then take the limit of this boundary to infinity.

In a box, the energy can be decomposed into the extrinsic energy and the residual force. In the general box, our energy as a function of the size of the box is given by \[ E ( r) = \frac{ \pi } { 2 } r \Lambda ^ 2 - \frac{ \pi } { 24 r } + \dots \] The first term represents the free space, non-observable energy. We can cancel this out by adding in a counterterm, which is a freely defined unnmeasurable additional term, which can be used in the intermediate steps to compute physical quanties.

Renormalising a scalar quantum field theory

Suppose we wanted to compute the cross section of two particles interacting in the theory where the Lagrangian looks like \[ L = - \frac{ 1 } { 2} \phi \box \phi - \frac{\lambda}{4! } \phi ^ 4 \] The second order correction to this term is infinite and diverges. It is a single loop diagram which diverges, and is a function of the four momentum going in. To make it manageable, we differentiate the integral then reintegrate it.

With the second order correction, our cross section is then \[ M ( s ) = - \lambda - \frac{ \lambda ^ 2 } { 32 \pi ^ 2 } \ln \frac { s } { \Lambda ^ 2 } \] We have an infinite quantity. We resolve this by realising that the observed coupling occuring at a particular centre of mass energy satisfies the relation, to first order \[ \lambda _R = \lambda + \frac{ \lambda ^ 2 } { 32 \pi ^ 2 } \ln \frac{ s_ 0 } { \Lambda ^ 2 } + \dots \] In this case, \( \lambda _ R \) is the renormalised observable quantity.

Once we write out this in terms of \( \lambda _ R \), we can now solve, and get an expression in terms of observable quantities.

To recap, we found that even though the energy of a system in total maybe infinite, we have a finite residual quantity. We foudn that even though the energy of this system was infinite and a function of our high energy cutoff, the residual phsyics popped out as finite. The high energy cutoff was required to restrict ultra high frequency radiation.
