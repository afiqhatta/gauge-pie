I’ve previously written about manifolds. Denis Sullivan won the Abel prize, for his work on dynamical systems and the topology of manifolds. What does it really mean to have the topology of a manifold? To understand this, we need to discuss some algebraic theory. In this post, I wanted to talk a little bit about ring theory. In particular, I wanted to discuss how we can turn a ring into a ‘localisation’, which is a weird mathematical object that looks like a fraction.

Localisation

A ring is an algebraic object that has multiplication and addition. More precisely, it is an abelian group that also has a multiplication operation that has an identity. For each of these operations, there is an identity element. The easiest and most ubiquitous example of a ring is the integers. Addition and multiplication are precisely those operations, and the identity elements are 0 and 1 respectively. 0 is the identity element for addition since a + 0 = a, and 1 is the identity element of the multiplicative operation since b x 1 = b.


Localisation is a way to have the notion of a ‘denominator’ for a given ring. It is a generalisation to the concept of fractions that we’ve learnt in primary school. To localise a ring, you first take a subset that doesn’t include the zero element - this is analogous to not being able to divide by zero. This subset also needs to be multiplicative, which means that any two elements in the subset multiplied together necessarily needs to belong in the subset.

If we take the integers as our ring, then there are a few examples of multiplicative subsets. The primes of Z do not form a multiplicative subset since two primes multiplied together do not form a prime. However, the set of non-primes do indeed form a multiplicative subset. The powers of two form a multiplicative subset of Z, and so do the odd numbers.


The localisation is then an equivalence class, where the equivalence relation is what you get when you multiply out two fractions together that are equal. The set of elements in a localisation are typically written in a fraction-type format.

The square brackets that are there show that the elements of S^-1 R are equivalence classes. Two elements are equivalent when you’d expect - it’s when the fraction can be ‘cancelled out’ into another fraction.
