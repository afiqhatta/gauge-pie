Magnetic Monopoles

In this post, I wanted to write about how the shapes of different spaces can dictate what kind of physical objects can exist. In particular, I wanted to talk about how we can construct magnetic monopoles in the correct geometric setting. In regular, classical electromagnetism, we refer to Maxwell’s equations to tell us what is, and what is not, physically possible. Owing to the fact that the divergence of a magnetic field is necessarily zero, a magnetic monopole cannot exist. The divergence of a magnetic field is what we get when we add up the partial derivative of the x-component with respect to x, the partial derivative of the y-component with respect to y, and so on.

Why is this true? A monopole requires non-zero flux coming out of it - it acts like a source. This can be shown fairly easily by considering what kind of flux a magnetic monopole would need to give out. Chern-Simons Theories

In the previous posts, I’ve discussed the concept of symmetry in physics. One particular group of symmetries that is easy to start with is the U(1) group, whose elements consists of complex numbers with unit length. The manifold that is associated with the U(1) group is the unit circle, which makes it a Lie group since the unit circle locally looks like one-dimensional Euclidean space. The most obvious theories that stem from a U(1) symmetry are electromagnetism and quantum electrodynamics. Among U(1) gauge theories, there are other interesting models that we can explore. All we really need is for the Lagrangian of the gauge potential A to exhibit some degree of invariance one way or another. Along the way, we might need to impose constraints.

It’s easy to show that the electromagnetic tensor is invariant under U(1) gauge transformations. In previous posts, I’ve discussed the Lagrangian for electromagnetism that gives rise to Maxwell’s equations. But, this is by no means the only model we can construct. One of the most interesting forms is that of the Chern Simons term, whose Lagrangian depends not on the electromagnetic tensor, but the gauge potential itself. Chern-Simons forms have a wide range of applicability in everything from topological quantum field theory to condensed matter physics.

Yang-Mills Theory

Yang-Mills theory is an SU(2) gauge theory. SU(2) is the Lie group of special unitary matrices of dimension two. If we attach SU(2) on the base space R^4, then we have a fibre bundle. R^4 is contractible, which implies the existence of just one gauge potential - not two like in the case of magnetic monopoles.
