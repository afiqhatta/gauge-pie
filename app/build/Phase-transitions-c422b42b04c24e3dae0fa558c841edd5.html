In the previous post, I discussed how the authors of [2] arrangedRubidium atoms in an array, and then excite these atoms to make a checkerboard of alternating energies. How does the state look like when it's transforming into the checkerboard phase? Is there some behaviour that is interesting at the limiting area when it collapses into a checkerboard state? These questions are answered by the study of phase transitions. The numbers that govern the power-law relationships between physically observable quantities are called critical exponents.


Correlation lengths

One of the quantities of interest is how the correlation lengths grow as our detuning increases, and the system evolves into the checkerboard phase. To do the experiment, we first start with all the atoms in the ground state. Then, the ratio ∆/Ω isincreased, and the correlation lengths are measured. The faster this ratio increases, the larger correlation lengths we see. There is a mechanism for how this works - it’s called the quantum Kibble-Zurek Mechanism. This mechanism describes how correlations break down when we’re near critical points. In the physical sciences, relaxation usually means the return of a perturbed system into equilibrium. For example, we might like to see how fast an oscillating spring returns to equilbirium after a quick impulse. Each relaxation process can be categorized by a relaxation time τ.



The Switch from an Ordered to a Disordered Phase

We want to study the transition from the disordered phase, to the ordered checkerboard phase. Before we go into the details of the actual experiment, I wanted to do a ‘simplified’ explanation assuming that we have a critical point when our order parameter is equal to zero. As the detuning goes from negative to positive, standard critical theory tells us to expect the relationship in the correlation length and the relaxation time to vary like the below. The greek Lambda in the case is our control parameter - the thing that we vary in the experiment (in this case it is ∆, but in general we can use any sort of control parameter).

The greek exponent nu here is called the critical exponent - it primarily describes how the correlations between atoms change as we move from a disordered state to an ordered state and vice versa. As the order parameter approaches zero, we find that the correlation length diverges - it implies that the local structure breaks down. Tau represents the relaxation time, and z merely is a scaling factor to relate the correlation length and the relaxation time.

Obtaining the Critical Point for Detuning

There is a critical value for our control parameter ∆, where there is a phase transition. This is the point where the susceptibility of the material is at its peak. The susceptibility of a material is defined as follows. On average, the proportion of atoms in the Rydberg state can be measured. The rate of change of this proportion versus the detuning is the susceptibility. Where the susceptibility reaches is peak, is where the critical point is.

Once we have the critical point for our detuning value, we can also measure the critical exponent nu. In this paper [3], it was measured to be 0.624. This measurement is in good agreement with the predicted ν = 0.629, that is in paper [1].
